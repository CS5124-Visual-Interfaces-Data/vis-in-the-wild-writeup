What is the purpose of this visualization?

This is a regularly updated map of all submarine cables in the world. These cables are fiber-optic, transferring 1 terabit per second. This allows subnets in different countries to communicate with each other, enabling the internet to run globally. that make up the internet. This visualization details approximate shapes of these cables on the seafloor, simplifying their curves.

Image source: David Fleetham / Alamy Stock Photo

The main purposes of this visualization are:
  • Education
  • Getting people on their mailing list here
  • Advertising for their custom visualization service
  • Promoting the geocoded data itself, purchasable here
  • Linking to the more complex version of this graphic, purchasable here

What is the data? How was the data captured or collected?

This is a properietary dataset owned by TeleGeography that you can get a license to here

Possible sources of data are the two sponsors for this visualization, AQUACOMMS and center3. Both of these companies own several submarine cables, and would know where their cables connected and lie on the seafloor.

Who are the users that this visualization was made for?

  • Laypeople who like pretty colors and lines (me)
  • Cable Enthusiaists
  • Companies interested in demoing their paid version
  • A datacenter planner figuring out where to build
  • Someone wanting to see regular updates to newly built cable lines

Questions & Insights

No statistics besides cable information is given, but the placement of landing points (the dots in the vis) are curious. They make me wonder about why certain areas have so many while others have none. A hypothesis is the landing points are influenced by population (people who would use the internet), but without more data I can't say for certain.

I also don't know why the cables are different colors (likely to differentiate) and why some cables split off into different colors, while still keeping the same name (example).

Comment on the visual and interaction design choices

The colors are fun, the lines are simple but convey location very well (despite not being perfectly exact).

Trying to select a specific cable is a nightmare. The hitboxes are very small and hard to pull up. There is no way to shift+select multiple cables at once. Instead, that needs to be done the ever-present sidebar.

What are the limitations of this design?

This design only shows the locations and landing points of submarine cables. Other information that would be cool as a non-submarine-cable-enthusiant like population or gdp stats are left out.

Half of the extra statistics given are not consistent or aren't specific:
  • Length is in the thousands of km (i.e. 25 000, 15 000, etc)
  • Dates give a month instead of a certain day or construction time frame (i.e. 2002 April)
  • Only a small number of cables have "Notes" detailing some interesting fun facts (example)
